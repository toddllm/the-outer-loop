Hypersonix flew its DART AE at Mach 8 - the first fully 3D-printed hydrogen-powered hypersonic aircraft.
Source →

What This Means

An Australian company flew the first aircraft that combines three frontier technologies: entirely 3D-printed construction, hydrogen fuel (zero carbon emissions), and hypersonic speed (Mach 8 = ~6,100 mph / ~9,800 km/h). For perspective, a flight from New York to London at Mach 8 would take about 35 minutes. 3D-printing the airframe dramatically cuts manufacturing cost and time. Hydrogen fuel means no carbon emissions. If this scales, it could reshape both military aviation and eventually commercial flight.

Starpath unveiled nanometer-thin space solar panels at 73 grams per square meter.
Source →

What This Means

Space solar panels that weigh only 73 grams per square meter - roughly the weight of a few sheets of paper. Current space solar panels weigh 10-50x more. Why weight matters in space: every gram costs money to launch. At SpaceX's current launch costs, lighter panels directly translate to cheaper space-based solar power. This is one of the enabling technologies for truly massive space solar farms, which could beam energy down to Earth or power orbital computing facilities.

SpaceX deployed 54 Starlink satellites in bicoastal launches in a single day.
Source →

What This Means

SpaceX launched from both US coasts in a single day, deploying 54 Starlink internet satellites. "Bicoastal" means separate launches from Cape Canaveral (Florida) and Vandenberg (California). Launches are becoming routine enough to run in parallel - this is space logistics, not space exploration. Think UPS, not NASA. Starlink now has 7,000+ satellites providing internet globally, with significant military applications (as seen with the Starlink-connected LUCAS drones mentioned in recent issues).
