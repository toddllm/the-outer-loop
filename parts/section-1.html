What This Means

OpenAI rushed to sign a Pentagon deal right after its rival Anthropic was banned from government use. The move backfired publicly - it looked like profiteering from a competitor's punishment rather than serving national security. So many users canceled their ChatGPT subscriptions in protest that Altman had to add Fourth Amendment privacy protections (limits on warrantless surveillance) to stop the bleeding.

What This Means

Despite being at odds with the Pentagon over surveillance, Anthropic still competed for a military contract - but drew a clear red line. Their proposal would let Claude handle drone fleet coordination (routing, logistics, formation) while explicitly refusing to let it decide what or who to attack. They drew the line at "autonomous targeting." They didn't win the contract, but the pitch itself is significant: even the AI company most openly opposed to military misuse is willing to do defense work, just with strict ethical boundaries.

3
Two AWS data centers in the UAE and one in Bahrain were hit by drones amid Iranian strikes - the first time military action has disrupted a major cloud provider.
Source →

What This Means

For the first time in history, a shooting war has physically damaged cloud computing infrastructure. AWS (Amazon Web Services) runs a significant portion of the world's internet services - everything from Netflix to government systems. The fact that military drones struck data centers means the boundary between "cyberwar" (attacking digital systems) and "real war" (physical destruction) has disappeared. Companies and governments that depend on cloud services now face a new category of risk: their servers can literally be bombed.

Israel deployed Iron Beam lasers in combat for the first time, intercepting Hezbollah rockets at $4 per shot versus $50,000 per missile.
Source →

What This Means

Israel used a laser defense system to shoot down incoming rockets in actual combat for the first time. The economics are staggering: a laser interception costs $4 in electricity. The current system uses a physical missile costing $50,000 per shot - that's 12,500x more expensive. If laser defense proves reliable at scale, it fundamentally changes the economics of warfare. Cheap rockets have been effective because they're cheaper than the missiles used to shoot them down. Lasers flip that equation entirely.
