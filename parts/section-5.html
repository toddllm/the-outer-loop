What This Means

You can now trade GPU computing power like oil futures on regulated exchanges. Ornn and Kalshi launched contracts letting you bet on (or hedge against) the future price of Nvidia H100 GPU time. "-regulated" means the US Commodity Futures Trading Commission oversees these markets, giving them the same legal legitimacy as corn or crude oil futures. Compute has officially graduated from "tech resource" to "commodity" - the same journey oil took in the 1980s. (Note: The author discloses a financial interest in Ornn.)

1
Go to kalshi.com and search for "GPU" or "H100." You'll see event contracts structured as yes/no bets on future compute prices - for example, "Will H100 spot price exceed $X by Q3 2026?" Each contract trades between $0.01 and $1.00, where the price reflects the market's probability estimate.
2
On ornn.com, the interface is more like a traditional futures exchange. You'll see H100 compute contracts with bid/ask spreads, settlement dates, and historical price charts - the same structure you'd find on a commodities exchange for oil or wheat. No account needed to browse prices.
3
What to look for: Compare spot prices (what compute costs now) vs. forward prices (what the market thinks it will cost in 6 months). If forwards trade higher, the market expects a compute shortage. If lower, supply is catching up to demand. This spread is now a tradeable insight.

Browsing is free. Account + KYC required to trade. Minimum trades start at ~$1.

What This Means

Xiaomi (one of the world's largest electronics companies - they make phones, TVs, scooters, and now cars) is testing its humanoid robot on an actual car production line. Three hours of continuous operation at 90%+ accuracy is a real benchmark, not a cherry-picked demo. For context: most factory robots today are bolted-down arms that repeat one motion. A humanoid that can move around the factory floor and handle varied tasks is a fundamentally different category of automation.
