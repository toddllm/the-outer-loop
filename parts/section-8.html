China's vehicles are now 12% electric, with fuel sales down 5.7%.
Source →

What This Means

One out of every eight vehicles on Chinese roads is now electric, and gasoline sales are already declining 5.7%. China is further along the EV transition than most people outside the country realize. Technology adoption follows an S-curve: slow at first, then explosive once it crosses ~10-15%. At 12%, China has crossed that inflection point. The decline in fuel sales isn't a forecast - it's already happening. This has implications for oil markets, geopolitics (less oil dependence), and the auto industry globally.

Podcasts have surpassed AM/FM talk radio in US spoken-word listening for the first time.
Source →

What This Means

More Americans now listen to spoken-word podcasts than traditional AM/FM talk radio. This is a structural shift that took about 20 years (podcasting started around 2004). The old broadcast model - a few voices reaching millions through licensed spectrum - has officially lost to the internet model: anyone can publish, listeners choose on demand. This matters because it's a pattern that repeats across media: newspapers → blogs, TV → YouTube, radio → podcasts. Each time, the gatekeepers lose and creators gain direct audience access.

Marc Andreessen reports that in Silicon Valley many people who favored government control of AI are now opposed to it.
Source →

What This Means

Marc Andreessen (co-founder of Netscape, co-founder of VC firm a16z, one of tech's most influential voices) says the mood in Silicon Valley has shifted. People who previously wanted the government to regulate AI are now opposed to government involvement. The likely driver: as AI becomes genuinely powerful, the Anthropic-Pentagon showdown (see Thread above) showed tech leaders what government control actually looks like in practice - demands for surveillance access and weapons integration. The abstract idea of "AI safety regulation" became concrete, and many didn't like what they saw.
